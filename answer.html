1. Architecture & Technical

Q1 What real-time tracking mechanism does the system use — WebSocket or polling? Why? ›

The system uses WebSocket via Socket.io, not polling. Polling would require every client to repeatedly ask the server "anything new?" — at scale during a flood event, that creates enormous unnecessary load. With WebSocket, a persistent bidirectional connection is maintained: the server pushes location and SOS updates to all relevant clients the moment they occur.

Socket.io was chosen specifically because it handles connection degradation gracefully — if a WebSocket connection drops, it automatically falls back to long-polling, which ensures continuity even in unstable network conditions common during floods.

Q2 What is the maximum acceptable latency between a user's actual location and its display on the map? ›

Our design target is under 2 seconds end-to-end from GPS capture on the device to pin update on the rescuer's map. In our MVP testing environment, observed latency was consistently under 2 seconds on a stable 4G connection. In degraded network conditions (2G/edge), latency increases but Socket.io's buffering ensures no update is silently lost — it queues and delivers when bandwidth allows.

For the SOS signal specifically, we treat this as a highest-priority message: it bypasses standard queue ordering and is pushed immediately to all connected rescuers and the command dashboard.

⚠ We are currently setting up formal latency benchmarking before our next release. The 2-second figure reflects observed behavior during MVP testing, not a formally measured SLA.
Q3 How does the system handle server overload when thousands of users are active simultaneously? ›

We use a three-layer defense: first, Redis as an in-memory cache absorbs read-heavy operations — location lookups and map state are served from Redis rather than hitting PostgreSQL on every request. Second, Socket.io with room-based broadcasting limits message fan-out: a rescuer only receives updates for their assigned zone, not the entire country. Third, our deployment on Render supports horizontal scaling — we can spin up additional instances under load.

For the current MVP phase, our infrastructure is sized for hundreds of concurrent users. Scaling to thousands is on our roadmap as we move toward production deployment with government partners.

Q4 What mechanism is used to send GPS data to the server — push or pull? At what frequency? ›

GPS data is push-based: the device proactively emits location updates through the persistent Socket.io connection. We do not poll the device from the server.

Update frequency is adaptive: while a rescuer is actively moving, location is emitted every 3–5 seconds. When a device is stationary for more than 30 seconds, the frequency drops to once per 30 seconds to conserve battery — critical for victims whose phones may be low on power. The last known position is always retained so the map never shows a rescuer as "disappeared."

Q5 If internet connectivity is lost, how does the app behave in offline mode? ›

We handle offline in two ways. First, local queuing: when a victim taps SOS with no connection, the alert is written immediately to local device storage. Socket.io automatically detects reconnection and flushes the queued SOS the moment any network signal is available — even briefly. The user sees "SOS Sent" immediately; they are not left waiting for server confirmation.

Second, the last known map state and tile cache are stored locally, so rescuers can still see the last recorded positions of victims and team members even without a live connection.

Additionally, we are developing a drone-based connectivity layer as a hardware complement: during severe flooding where all cell towers are compromised, lightweight drones can be deployed over the affected area broadcasting a local Wi-Fi network. Devices within range connect to this mesh and SOS signals route through the drone relay to reach command centers. This is currently in the planning and prototyping phase, and we are in early discussions with drone operators and civil defense agencies about deployment protocols.

The drone connectivity layer is a planned extension. Current MVP relies on local queuing + auto-retry on reconnect.
Q6 Does the backend scale horizontally or vertically? Why? ›

Our architecture is designed for horizontal scaling. The Node.js + Express backend is stateless — session state lives in Redis, not in the application server itself. This means we can run multiple identical server instances behind a load balancer, adding capacity without reconfiguring the application.

Vertical scaling (bigger single server) hits a ceiling and creates a single point of failure — unacceptable for a life-safety system. Horizontal scaling also allows zero-downtime deployments: we roll instances one at a time while others continue serving traffic.

2. Data & Flood Map

Q7 Where does the real-time flood data come from? ›

We use a three-source fusion model. The primary source is the Vietnam Meteorology and Hydrology Administration (VNMHA) API, which provides official water level and flood alert data. This is authoritative, institutionally verified data updated on a regular government schedule.

This is supplemented by IoT sensor data from water-level measurement devices deployed at flood-prone locations. These provide more granular, real-time readings between government update cycles.

The third layer is crowdsourced reports from users on the ground — rescuers and residents can flag new flood zones, road blockages, or water level changes directly in the app. These reports are displayed with a visual confidence indicator distinguishing them from sensor-verified data, so rescuers can calibrate their trust in each data point.

Q8 What factors determine the accuracy of the live flood map? How is the margin of error assessed? ›

Accuracy depends on three factors: sensor density (more IoT devices = finer spatial resolution), data freshness (government API update intervals vs. sensor polling rate), and report validation (crowdsourced data is weighted by the number of independent sources confirming the same area).

We display a confidence score on each flood zone marker: green for sensor-verified + government-confirmed, amber for sensor-only, red for user-reported without cross-validation. This allows rescuers to make informed routing decisions based on data quality, not just data presence.

⚠ Formal accuracy benchmarking against ground truth is on our roadmap. Current confidence scoring is rule-based, not ML-derived.
Q9 When flood map data and a victim's GPS coordinates contradict each other, which does the system prioritize? ›

The victim's live GPS position is always authoritative for dispatch purposes. The flood map shows the environment; the GPS shows where the person actually is. If a victim's pin appears in a zone the map shows as safe, we flag this as a data discrepancy alert on the command dashboard — prompting operators to investigate rather than silently accepting either source.

In practice, this discrepancy usually means the flood has advanced faster than the map has updated, which itself is valuable real-time intelligence: the system logs these contradictions and uses them to trigger an expedited map refresh in that zone.

Q10 How frequently is the map updated? Is that fast enough for sudden flash flood events? ›

Government API data is updated on the agency's own schedule — typically every 15–30 minutes. IoT sensor data is polled every 60 seconds. Crowdsourced reports appear on the map in real time the moment a user submits them.

For flash floods, the crowdsourced and IoT layers are the critical ones — rescuers and residents on the ground will always outpace government update cycles during a rapid-onset event. Our system is explicitly designed around this reality: the crowdsource layer is the "early warning" tier, the sensor layer is the "confirmation" tier, and government data is the authoritative baseline.

Q11 How is historical flood data stored and used to improve future predictions? ›

All flood events, SOS locations, rescue outcomes, and sensor readings are stored with timestamps in PostgreSQL with geospatial indexing. This builds a historical dataset of which zones flood first, how quickly water rises, and what routes rescuers took.

In the current MVP, this data is used for post-event analysis and reporting. Our roadmap includes integrating this historical dataset with a predictive model to surface early risk scores for areas showing early indicators — giving rescue teams pre-positioning intelligence before the first SOS is sent.

3. UX & Practical Use

Q12 Flood-affected residents often use old phones with low battery — how is the app optimized for those constraints? ›

Battery and device performance are first-class constraints in our design. Key optimizations include: adaptive GPS polling (frequency drops when stationary), lightweight map tile caching (no re-downloading tiles already seen), and a low-data mode that strips non-essential UI when bandwidth is limited.

The iOS app is built in Swift, which gives us close control over system resources — we avoid background processes that drain battery when the app is minimized. The web version on React is similarly optimized: only the map component and SOS button remain active in crisis mode; secondary features are deferred.

Q13 How can a panicking victim still use the app within 10 seconds? ›

The SOS action is the largest, most prominent element on the victim's home screen — a single large button requiring one tap plus a 2-second hold to prevent accidental triggers. No menu navigation, no form, no login required to send the alert. The hold-to-confirm mechanism is the only friction we intentionally introduce.

Once the SOS is sent, the victim immediately sees: a confirmation message, the number of rescuers notified, and a live indicator showing their signal is active. This "your message was received" feedback is critical for someone in a panicked state.

Q14 Are the interfaces for rescue teams and victims different? How is role-based access managed? ›

Yes — completely different interfaces for different roles. The victim interface is minimal: SOS button, basic location indicator, status of their rescue request. The rescuer interface shows the live map with all victim pins, their own assigned cases, route suggestions, and team communication. The command dashboard (web, ReactJS) shows the full operational picture: all active SOSes, all rescue teams, zone coverage, and unattended cases.

Role-based access is enforced via JWT tokens with role claims. A victim token cannot access rescuer endpoints; a rescuer token cannot access admin functions. Account roles are assigned at registration and can only be changed by an administrator.

Q15 Can illiterate users or elderly residents in rural areas use the app? ›

The victim SOS flow is designed to be icon-first, not text-first. The SOS button uses universally understood visual language — a large red distress symbol — with minimal text labels. Voice guidance is on our roadmap for the next release.

For users who cannot operate a smartphone at all, our system supports proxy SOS submission: a family member or neighbor can submit a rescue request on their behalf by dropping a pin on the map. We recognize this is not a complete solution for all elderly users, and it is one of the reasons we are actively pursuing integration with local civil defense networks where trained volunteers can assist.

Q16 Has the app been tested with real users in a simulated flood environment? ›

Yes. We conducted MVP testing with a group of volunteer users simulating flood victim and rescuer roles in a field environment. Testers used the app under deliberately degraded conditions — throttled network, low battery devices, and time pressure — to surface usability issues before wider deployment.

Key findings from testing informed our current design: the hold-to-confirm SOS mechanism was added after accidental triggers were observed, and the adaptive GPS polling was tuned based on real battery drain measurements during the session.

Q17 What channel is used to send an SOS alert when internet is unavailable? ›

Currently, the SOS is queued locally and sent as soon as any internet connection is restored — this is the primary fallback. The system uses Socket.io's built-in reconnection logic with exponential backoff to retry delivery automatically.

We are actively developing a secondary channel: our drone-based Wi-Fi relay system. Drones deployed over flood zones create a local mesh network that devices can connect to without cellular infrastructure, routing SOS signals through the drone to satellite or ground relay stations. This hardware layer is in prototyping and we are working with partners on regulatory clearance for disaster-zone drone operations.

4. Safety, Security & Privacy

Q18 Who has access to a victim's location data? Is there a risk of bad actors exploiting it? ›

A victim's live location is visible only to: verified rescue team members assigned to or operating in the victim's zone, and command center operators with admin credentials. Unauthenticated users, other victims, and unverified volunteers cannot access any location data.

Victim locations are never displayed on a public-facing map. They exist only within authenticated sessions, transmitted over HTTPS/TLS, and are not indexed or searchable outside the command system. After a rescue is completed or the event closes, location data is archived and access is restricted to post-event analysis by authorized administrators.

Q19 How is location data encrypted in transit and at rest? ›

In transit: all API communication uses HTTPS with TLS 1.2+. Socket.io connections are established over WSS (WebSocket Secure). No location data is ever transmitted over unencrypted channels.

At rest: location data is stored in PostgreSQL on an encrypted volume. Database credentials are environment-injected (not hardcoded), and access is restricted to the application service account only. Redis cache entries for live location data have short TTLs and are not persisted to disk.

Q20 If someone impersonates a rescuer to take advantage of victims, is there a detection mechanism? ›

Rescuer accounts go through an admin approval workflow before activation. New registrations claiming rescuer status are flagged as pending and cannot access victim locations until a human administrator reviews and approves them. We also require OTP verification of the registrant's phone number, ensuring each account is tied to a real, reachable identity.

Additionally, victims can see the verified badge and name of the rescuer assigned to them, and can report concerns through the app. Command operators monitor active assignments and can revoke rescuer access instantly if suspicious behavior is flagged.

⚠ Upload of official credentials (ID, civil defense certification) is on our roadmap as a stronger verification step beyond OTP + admin approval.
Q21 After the disaster, how is victims' personal data handled? Is it ever sold or shared? ›

Personal data is never sold or shared with third parties for commercial purposes. After an event concludes, personally identifiable information (name, phone number, precise location history) is separated from operational data: aggregated statistics are retained for analysis and reporting; individual PII is archived with restricted access and a defined retention period.

We are aligning our data handling practices with Vietnam's Personal Data Protection Decree (Decree 13/2023/ND-CP), which governs collection, processing, and storage of personal data. Data shared with government partners is done under formal data sharing agreements that prohibit secondary use.

Q22 Which data protection regulations does the app comply with? ›

Our primary compliance framework is Vietnam's Decree 13/2023/ND-CP on Personal Data Protection, which came into force in 2023 and is the most directly applicable regulation for a Vietnam-deployed application. This covers consent requirements, data minimization, purpose limitation, and subject rights.

We have also reviewed GDPR principles as a design reference for data minimization and retention policies, as it represents international best practice. We are not currently required to comply with GDPR as we do not serve EU residents, but applying its principles raises our standard above the regulatory minimum.

5. Deployment & Operations

Q23 What is the monthly operating cost? Who pays? What is the financial model? ›

At current MVP scale, infrastructure costs (Render hosting, database, Cloudinary media storage) are in the range of $50–150/month — manageable as a team-funded project. As we scale to production and add drone infrastructure, costs will increase significantly.

Our target financial model is B2G (Business-to-Government): the primary paying customer is provincial and national civil defense agencies who license the platform as part of their emergency response infrastructure. This model is appropriate because: the government has the mandate, the budget, and the existing rescue teams to deploy the system effectively. We are also exploring NGO and international humanitarian organization partnerships (e.g. Red Cross Vietnam, UNDP) as grant funding sources during the scale-up phase.

We are currently in active conversations with investors and are seeking seed funding to bring the system to production-grade deployment.

Q24 How would the team convince local governments to integrate this system? ›

Our go-to-government strategy has three steps. First, demonstrated outcomes: we run a controlled pilot with one province's civil defense unit, document response time improvements and coordination gains, and use that data as the sales case for neighboring provinces. Government procurement is evidence-driven.

Second, integration, not replacement: we position the system as a layer on top of existing civil defense workflows, not a replacement for them. The command dashboard is designed to complement, not compete with, existing dispatch processes. Rescuers keep their current radio and phone systems; our app adds coordination visibility they don't currently have.

Third, low onboarding friction: we can deploy and train a rescue team in under half a day. The system doesn't require new hardware beyond smartphones that teams already carry.

Q25 How long does it take to train a rescue team to use the app proficiently? ›

Based on our MVP testing, a rescue team member reaches basic operational proficiency — able to receive assignments, navigate to victims, and update status — in under 2 hours. Command dashboard operators, who have a more complex interface, typically need a half-day session.

We provide a structured onboarding: a 30-minute guided walkthrough, followed by a simulated exercise where the team runs a practice rescue on a test environment with seeded victim data. This hands-on simulation is more effective than documentation alone.

Q26 Can the system be expanded to other disaster types — wildfires, earthquakes? ›

Yes. The core architecture — real-time location tracking, SOS dispatch, live map overlays, command dashboard — is disaster-agnostic. The flood-specific elements are the data sources (flood sensor APIs, water level layers) and some UX copy. These are modular and replaceable.

A wildfire deployment would substitute fire perimeter APIs and smoke spread data as the map layer. An earthquake deployment would replace flood zones with structural damage reports and aftershock probability zones. The rescue coordination logic is identical across all scenarios. Expanding to a second disaster type is a configuration and data-integration task, not a rebuild.

Q27 Who manages and verifies official rescuer accounts? ›

In the current MVP, our team acts as platform administrator, manually reviewing and approving rescuer registrations. For production deployment, this role transfers to the government agency or civil defense unit operating the system in their province — they become the account authority for their jurisdiction, with their own admin dashboard to manage their team's accounts.

This federated model means each province controls their own verified rescuer roster, and we as the platform provider do not create a central bottleneck or hold authority over government personnel decisions.

Q28 If the project loses funding after six months, how would the data and system be handled? ›

We take data continuity seriously as a responsibility to users who trusted us with their information. Our commitment: if we cease operations, all victim and rescue data would be exported and transferred to the relevant government agency in a standard, open format (CSV/GeoJSON). No data would be deleted without giving authorities the opportunity to archive it.

We are also designing the system to be self-hostable — a government agency or NGO could run the entire stack on their own infrastructure with minimal dependency on us. This is a deliberate architectural choice to prevent lock-in and ensure long-term operational continuity regardless of our company's status.

6. Edge Cases & Challenges

Q29 What happens when two rescue teams are dispatched to the same victim while another victim receives no help? ›

Our dispatch system uses a claim-and-lock mechanism: once a rescuer accepts an SOS assignment, that victim's pin is marked as "assigned" and removed from the open queue visible to other teams. A second team cannot claim the same victim unless the first team explicitly releases the assignment or a timeout occurs.

The command dashboard shows real-time assignment status for every active SOS: unassigned (red), assigned (amber), en route (blue), completed (green). A command operator can see gaps instantly and manually re-assign unattended cases if needed.

Q30 If a victim does not have a phone, does the system offer any support? ›

Yes — through proxy SOS submission. A family member, neighbor, or any connected user can submit a rescue request on behalf of someone without a phone by entering an address or dropping a pin on the map. The submission includes a notes field where the submitter can describe the person's situation, number of people, and any special needs.

We also recognize that not all victims will be reached digitally. Our system is designed to complement, not replace, radio and phone dispatch. If a civil defense operator receives an analog call reporting a victim's location, they can manually enter that location into the command dashboard and dispatch a team from there — the same as they would for any digitally submitted SOS.

Q31 When flooding cuts off an area with no mobile coverage, is the app still useful? ›

In a complete blackout scenario, the app's live features require connectivity to function. However, two things remain available offline: the cached map tiles from the last sync (rescuers can still see the geographic area), and the last known positions of tracked individuals stored locally.

For true no-signal scenarios, our drone relay initiative directly addresses this gap. Beyond that, we advocate for pre-positioning: in the hours before a flood peaks, teams use the system to understand the at-risk population distribution, pre-identify high-risk households, and plan routes — so that even if connectivity drops at peak flood, teams already have a mission plan from when the system was online.

Q32 How does the system distinguish a real SOS from an error or spam trigger? ›

Three layers of anti-false-alarm design. First, hold-to-confirm: the SOS requires a 2-second press, not a tap — accidental triggers are the most common source of false alerts and this eliminates most of them. Second, a 10-second cancellation window immediately after submission lets the sender abort if they triggered accidentally. Third, rate limiting per account: a user cannot submit more than 3 SOS signals in 10 minutes from the same account — flagged signals go into a review queue rather than auto-dispatching.

Crowdsourced flood reports use a minimum confirmation threshold: a single user's flood report is shown as "unverified" until at least two independent users confirm the same zone, at which point it is promoted to the map.

Q33 If the central server goes down at the peak of a flood event, what is the contingency plan? ›

Three-tier contingency. Tier 1: SOS requests queued on device are retried automatically — the server going down does not lose already-submitted alerts, they are held locally and re-sent when the server recovers. Tier 2: Render's infrastructure supports automatic instance restart and we maintain a warm standby configuration that activates within minutes. Tier 3: the human fallback — command operators revert to radio and phone coordination, which they are trained for independent of our system. Our app enhances their capability; it does not replace their baseline competency.

We are adding uptime monitoring with automated alerting (PagerDuty or equivalent) so the team is notified of outages within 60 seconds, even at 3 AM during a flood event.

7. Comparison & Innovation

Q34 How is this app different from Zalo or Facebook, which people already use to call for help? ›

Zalo and Facebook are communication platforms — they are excellent at broadcasting a message to your social network. But they have no mechanism to coordinate a response. A victim posting on Zalo creates awareness; our app creates a dispatch. The core differences:

Verified rescuers: Zalo has no way to know who is a real rescue team versus a well-meaning bystander. Our system only connects victims to credentialed, admin-approved responders. Structured dispatch: Facebook has no assignment system — two people might go to the same victim while ten others are missed. Our claim-and-lock dispatch prevents duplication and tracks coverage gaps. Live operational map: social platforms have no real-time location layer showing all victims and all rescuers simultaneously. Our command dashboard gives coordinators situational awareness that Zalo simply cannot provide. Offline SOS: if a victim's phone has no signal, no Zalo message is sent. Our queued SOS is delivered the moment any connectivity returns.

Q35 Are there similar solutions in Japan, Thailand, or Bangladesh? What improvements does this app offer? ›

Similar systems exist: Japan's J-Alert and Yahoo! Bosai focus on early warning and evacuation routing but not real-time victim rescue coordination. Bangladesh's CPP (Cyclone Preparedness Programme) uses trained volunteers but relies on radio, not digital coordination. Thailand's DDPM has a reporting app but lacks live rescuer-victim tracking.

Our differentiator is the combination of victim tracking + rescuer tracking + live flood map + coordinated dispatch in a single platform. Existing tools address one or two of these; we address all of them together. Additionally, our drone-relay connectivity layer is, to our knowledge, novel in the humanitarian tech space for flood response specifically.

Q36 Why is a dedicated app needed instead of integrating into existing 113/114 emergency services? ›

We see ourselves as complementary to 113/114, not competing. 113/114 are voice-based dispatch systems designed for individual incidents — one call, one response. A flood event involves hundreds of simultaneous victims across a geographic area, which overwhelms voice-based triage.

Our app provides what 113/114 cannot: a visual operational map, multi-team coordination, priority queuing across dozens of simultaneous cases, and victim self-reporting without tying up phone lines. We are actively interested in integrating with 113/114 as a referral channel — calls that come in during a flood event could be entered into our system by the 114 operator and dispatched digitally, combining their established trust with our coordination capability.

Q37 Does the system use AI or ML to predict which areas will be at risk next? ›

Not in the current MVP — we want to be transparent about this. The current system is real-time reactive: it shows what is happening now based on sensor, government, and user data. Predictive capability is a planned next phase.

Our historical data layer is being built with this in mind: every flood event is stored with timestamped geospatial records of how water spread, which zones flooded in which order, and what weather conditions preceded the event. Once we have sufficient historical coverage (target: 2–3 flood seasons), we intend to train a gradient-boosted or LSTM model to output risk probability scores for zones 2–6 hours ahead of expected inundation. This gives rescue teams pre-positioning intelligence before the first SOS arrives.

Q38 In three years, what direction does the team see this app evolving toward? ›

Three clear directions. Geographic expansion: from Vietnam's Mekong Delta and Central Coast to other flood-vulnerable countries in Southeast Asia — Bangladesh, Myanmar, Philippines — where the need is acute and government digital infrastructure is similar. Predictive intelligence: from reactive coordination to proactive pre-positioning, using our growing historical dataset to tell rescue teams where to be before victims call. Hardware integration: making the drone connectivity layer a productized, deployable kit that civil defense agencies can operate themselves, not something that requires our team on site.

The vision is to become the coordination operating system for disaster response in Southeast Asia — not one app, but a platform that government agencies, NGOs, and community responders all plug into.

Answers from the perspective of actual end users — honest, grounded, emotionally aware.

1. Flood Residents

Q1After a victim sends an SOS, how long does it take for someone to arrive? Critical›

The SOS reaches verified rescue teams within seconds of being sent — it is pushed immediately via WebSocket to all rescuers in the area and to the command dashboard. How quickly a team physically arrives depends on the distance and road conditions, which vary by situation. What we can guarantee is that the alert is received instantly, and the command dashboard ensures no alert goes unassigned. The victim sees a status update — "Your SOS was received. A team is being assigned" — immediately after sending.

Q2How does a victim know the rescue team is on the way? High›

Once a rescuer accepts the assignment, the victim's app updates in real time: they see the rescuer's name, their verified badge, and a live dot showing the team's current position moving toward them on the map. Status labels progress: SOS Received → Rescuer Assigned → En Route → Arrived. The victim is never left in silence wondering if anyone is coming.

Q3If the victim's phone dies or loses signal, will their SOS still be received? High›

If the SOS was sent before the phone died or lost signal, it has already been delivered to the server and to rescue teams — the victim's phone being off afterward does not affect that. If the phone loses signal while sending, the SOS is saved locally and retried the moment any connection returns. If the phone dies completely before sending, the SOS cannot be sent from that device — which is why we support proxy SOS submission from a family member's phone, and why we recommend victims send their SOS as early as possible rather than waiting until the last moment.

Q4What if the map shows the area as safe while water is already rising? High›

This is a real risk with any map that relies on periodic data updates. Our response is two-fold: first, we clearly mark each flood zone with a confidence level and last-updated timestamp, so a rescuer or victim can see "this zone was confirmed flooded 8 minutes ago by 3 reports" rather than treating all map data as equally current. Second, when a victim's GPS position contradicts the map (they're in a zone marked safe but have sent an SOS), the system flags this as a priority discrepancy and treats the victim's live report as ground truth.

Q5How does a victim verify the person coming is a real rescuer, not an impersonator? Medium›

The victim's app displays the assigned rescuer's verified name, photo, and badge number before they arrive. When the rescuer arrives, the victim can match the person at the door to the profile shown in the app. Rescuers also carry their civil defense identification which victims can ask to see. This two-factor verification — digital identity in app + physical ID — is our current standard. We are exploring QR-code based identity confirmation as an additional layer in the next release.

Q6If an area has completely lost network coverage, can victims still use the app? High›

In a complete blackout, the SOS is queued locally and will send automatically when any signal returns. Victims can also see the last-cached map and the last known positions of rescue teams near them. For true zero-signal scenarios, our drone relay initiative is the longer-term answer — but we are honest that this is in development, not deployed yet. In the meantime, we strongly recommend that high-risk communities also maintain analog fallbacks (local volunteer networks, radio, visual distress signals) alongside the app.

Q7Does the app run on older, low-end smartphones? Practical›

The web app (ReactJS) is designed to be lightweight and runs on Android devices from 2018 onward on Chrome or Firefox. The iOS app (Swift) supports iPhone 7 and newer, which covers most phones in active use in Vietnam today. We intentionally avoid heavy animations, large bundle sizes, and background processes that degrade performance on older hardware. In our MVP testing, the app ran acceptably on devices with 2GB RAM and Android 8.0.

Q8Is the app available in local dialects or minority languages? Practical›

Currently the app is in Vietnamese and English. Adding minority language support — Khmer, Ede, Thai, Tày, and others spoken in flood-prone highland and delta communities — is on our localization roadmap. In the near term, we compensate with an icon-first design for the core SOS flow: the critical action (send SOS) does not require reading any text. Full language support is a priority for our next major release.

Q9Does a victim need to create an account before sending an SOS? Medium›

No account is required to send a basic SOS. A victim can open the app, tap SOS, and their GPS location is transmitted immediately — no registration, no login. An optional account allows victims to add their name, household size, and medical needs, which helps rescuers arrive better prepared. We explicitly designed it this way: registration is a feature, not a gate.

2. Rescue Teams

Q10Does the app suggest the best route, accounting for flooded roads? Important›

Yes. Routing is calculated using Google Maps API with dynamic road exclusions: roads that have been marked as flooded or impassable — by our IoT data layer, government reports, or user submissions — are excluded from route calculations. The rescuer sees a route that avoids known flood zones, not just the shortest path. When a rescuer reports a new blockage mid-route, the system immediately recalculates an alternative and notifies the command dashboard.

Q11When 50 SOSes arrive simultaneously, how does the system prevent duplication and gaps? Important›

The dispatch system uses claim-and-lock assignment combined with proximity-based clustering. When a rescuer accepts a case, it is locked to them and removed from the open queue. The system suggests routes that allow one team to reach multiple nearby victims in sequence — reducing the total number of runs needed. The command dashboard shows uncovered cases in real time so operators can see and address gaps as they emerge. No SOS stays unassigned for more than a configurable timeout before it auto-escalates to the command operator's attention.

Q12After a rescue, how does a team member log the outcome without heavy data entry? Practical›

Stress-Test Questions

Answers for combined-failure scenarios — the hardest questions at any demo or pitch.

⚠ COMBINED FAILURE — multiple systems under stress simultaneously
Stress-test AEverything fails at once
3 AM. Server at 98% CPU. App crashing. 200 SOSes. Towers down.
Q1What does the system do when the server reaches capacity? Critical›

Three layers activate in sequence. Layer 1 — device queue: SOS requests are already on the device before the server sees them; a full server doesn't lose them, it just delays acknowledgment. Layer 2 — Redis buffer: incoming requests hit Redis first, which can handle tens of thousands of operations per second — far more than PostgreSQL alone. Redis acts as a shock absorber, queuing writes and serving reads from cache while the primary database recovers. Layer 3 — Render auto-scaling: when CPU sustained above 80% for 2 minutes, Render spins up an additional instance and the load balancer distributes traffic across both. This happens without manual intervention and without dropping active WebSocket connections.

Q2If the app crashes on a rescuer's phone mid-mission, does their assigned victim stay assigned? Critical›

Yes — assignment state lives on the server, not the device. The rescuer's JWT token contains their identity; when the app restarts and reconnects, the server restores their active assignment automatically. The victim's SOS remains in "assigned" state and the rescuer can resume navigating without any action from an operator.

However, if the app crash also causes the rescuer to stop moving (they put the phone down to reboot it, for instance), the movement-based escalation timer starts: after 15 minutes stationary, the system sends a dual-channel alert. If the rescuer responds — by reopening the app and tapping confirm — the assignment continues normally. If they do not respond within 5 minutes, the system auto-reassigns to the nearest available team and flags the original rescuer for a welfare check. This ensures a crashed phone never silently orphans a victim.

Q3What data is preserved locally if the server becomes unreachable? High›

Locally preserved: active assignment details (victim name, last known location, notes), cached map tiles for the area the rescuer was last viewing, queued outbound messages (status updates, location pings), and the last 30 minutes of victim position history in the area. Not preserved offline: live location updates for other rescuers and real-time command dashboard data — these require connectivity. The rescuer can continue navigating to their assigned victim using the last known position and cached map.

Q4How does the monitoring team know the platform is degraded, and what is the manual protocol? High›

We are implementing uptime monitoring with automated alerting (target: PagerDuty) that triggers within 60 seconds of any endpoint failure or response time exceeding 5 seconds. On-call engineers are notified via SMS and push regardless of time of day. The manual escalation protocol is documented: first responder assesses and attempts automated recovery (restart instance), escalates to lead engineer if unresolved in 10 minutes, and notifies government operators to activate their radio-based backup coordination protocol if system downtime exceeds 15 minutes.

Stress-test BBad data corrupts the map

Q5What prevents false flood reports from corrupting the live map? Critical›

Three validation layers. Source weighting: government API and IoT sensor data are weighted as authoritative; single user reports are shown as "unverified" and do not alter routing recommendations until cross-validated. Consensus threshold: a flood zone is not promoted to "confirmed" on crowdsourced data alone until at least 2 independent accounts report it within a time window. Contradiction detection: if a user report directly contradicts a sensor reading from the same area, the system flags the conflict on the command dashboard and freezes that zone's classification pending manual review rather than accepting the more recent report uncritically.

Q6How quickly can an admin roll back bad map data? High›

An administrator can select any time window and revert the map state to a previous snapshot in under 60 seconds. All changes to sensor-verified zones require a second admin to confirm, preventing a single compromised account from corrupting authoritative data.

Yes — every flood zone marker has a confidence indicator: solid fill for sensor/government verified, hatched fill for user-reported-pending-confirmation, and a distinct color band showing data age. Rescuers can tap any zone to see: data source, last updated timestamp, number of reports, and any contradiction flags. This is not a cosmetic feature — it is how rescuers decide whether to trust a route recommendation. ⚠ COMBINED FAILURE — operational silence with a life at stake
Stress-test CSilent rescue — 4 hours, no update

Q8Does the system escalate when a rescue has been "in progress" too long with no update? Critical›

Yes — we use a two-stage inactivity escalation protocol triggered by GPS movement analysis, not just a time countdown.

Stage 1 — Alert (T+15 min stationary): The system continuously tracks the rescuer's GPS position. If a rescuer assigned to an active case has not moved more than a defined threshold for 15 minutes, the system flags this as anomalous — a rescuer en route should be moving. An immediate dual-channel alert fires: an in-app warning banner on the rescuer's screen ("Are you still on mission? Tap to confirm") and a push notification + SMS to their registered number simultaneously. This dual channel ensures the alert reaches them even if the app is backgrounded or the screen is off.

Stage 2 — Auto-reassignment (T+20 min, i.e. 5 min after alert): If the rescuer does not respond to the Stage 1 alert within 5 minutes — no tap, no status update, no movement — the system automatically releases the case and assigns it to the nearest available rescuer. The victim receives an immediate push notification: "We're sending a new rescue team to you. Help is still coming." The original rescuer's case is flagged on the command dashboard as "silent — investigate," triggering a separate protocol to check on their wellbeing.

The 15-minute movement threshold is calibrated to avoid false positives — a rescuer loading a victim onto a boat or navigating on foot may briefly stop. The combination of movement detection + dual-channel alert + 5-minute response window is designed to catch genuine problems without disrupting active rescues.

Q9Can a command operator check the last known GPS position of the silent rescue team? High›

Yes — the command dashboard maintains a full GPS movement trail for every active rescuer, retained for the duration of the event. An operator can open any rescuer's profile and see their path drawn on the map: where they departed, what route they took, where their movement stopped, and the exact timestamp of their last recorded position.

This gives the operator three critical pieces of intelligence: did the team reach the victim's area at all (they may have been blocked early), are they stationary at the victim's location (potentially on scene and performing a rescue without connectivity), or did they stop somewhere in between (potential difficulty). The trail is the basis for deciding the correct response — send a second rescue team to the victim, dispatch a welfare check to the rescuer's last known position, or both.

Q10If a rescue team goes completely silent, does the system flag them as potentially in distress too? High›

Yes — this is what we call dual-tracking. Every active rescuer is monitored the same way as every active victim. A rescue team that goes silent triggers the same escalation protocol: amber warning, red alert, SMS to their contact. The command dashboard separates "team silent in the field" from "victim SOS unanswered" but treats both with the same urgency. Rescuers can also proactively send a "I'm OK — no update needed" ping with one tap to reset the silence timer without changing their status — useful when they're on scene and focused on the rescue.

Jury Challenge 3 GPS vs battery

Citizen & Victim — How-to & Real-Use Questions

Questions that citizens, flood victims, and the general public ask when they first encounter the app — practical, emotional, and experience-driven.

Registration & Getting Started

Q1Do I need to create an account before I can send an SOS?›

Yes — citizens register an account before using the app. Registration takes under 2 minutes: enter your name, phone number, receive an OTP, and you're in. We require registration (unlike a fully anonymous flow) because it gives rescuers critical information — your name, a photo to identify you, and your household profile — that makes the rescue faster and safer. A rescuer arriving at a flood scene with your name and photo can confirm they have the right person and the right address.

Registration also allows the system to send you status notifications about your SOS and lets family members track your rescue in real time. We recognize registration adds 2 minutes of friction upfront — which is why we strongly encourage people in flood-prone areas to register before a flood event, not during one.

Preparedness messaging: download and register during dry season. The 2-minute registration becomes a problem only if it's done in a panic at rising water.
Q2I've never used this app before and my house is flooding right now. What do I do first?›

If you do not have an account yet: download the app, tap "Register," enter your name and phone number, enter the OTP sent to your phone, then tap the large red SOS button. That is 4 steps. We have timed this: a first-time user who can read the screen can complete it in under 90 seconds on a working phone.

Once SOS is sent, you will see a confirmation screen telling you your alert has been received. Stay where you are if it is safe to do so — moving makes it harder for rescuers to find you. Keep your phone charged and the app open if possible so your location stays live.

If you cannot register in time — because your phone is too wet, your hands are shaking, or you have no signal — ask a family member in a dry location to submit a proxy SOS for you with your address.

Q3What information does the app collect about me when I register?›

Registration collects: your name, phone number, and optionally a profile photo. During an active SOS, your live GPS location is transmitted to verified rescue teams and the command dashboard only. After the rescue is complete, location tracking stops automatically.

Optional household profile fields — number of people in your home, whether any are elderly or children, and any medical conditions — are never required but dramatically help rescuers prepare the right equipment before arrival. You can fill these in at any time from your profile screen, ideally before flood season.

We do not share your personal data with advertisers, third-party companies, or any party outside the rescue operation. Full details are in our privacy policy, which complies with Vietnam's Decree 13/2023/ND-CP on personal data protection.

Q4Can I use the app on an old Android phone from 2018?›

Yes. The web app runs on Chrome or Firefox on any Android device running Android 8.0 or newer — which covers the vast majority of phones still in use in Vietnam today, including budget devices with 2GB RAM. The iOS native app supports iPhone 7 and newer.

We specifically tested on low-end devices during our MVP phase and optimized for them: no heavy animations, lightweight map tiles, and a minimal-data mode that strips non-essential UI when bandwidth is slow. If your phone is too old or too slow to run the app, the proxy SOS flow — where a family member submits a request on your behalf — remains available as a fallback.

Sending SOS & What Happens Next

Q5I tapped SOS. How do I know someone actually received it?›

Immediately after you hold the SOS button for 2 seconds and release, the screen shows a confirmation: "Your SOS has been received. Rescue teams in your area have been notified." This confirmation only appears once the server has acknowledged the alert — it is not a local fake confirmation.

Within minutes, your status updates to show: which rescue team has been assigned to you, the rescuer's name and verified badge, and a live dot on the map showing the team moving toward your location. You will also receive a push notification when a team is assigned. If no team is assigned within 5 minutes, the system automatically flags your case on the command dashboard for manual operator attention.

Q6I sent SOS by accident. How do I cancel it?›

After sending an SOS, a 10-second cancellation window appears on screen with a prominent "Cancel — this was a mistake" button. If you tap it within 10 seconds, the alert is withdrawn and no rescue team is dispatched. After 10 seconds, the SOS is confirmed and enters the dispatch queue — at that point, tap "Cancel my SOS" from the status screen, which notifies the assigned team and releases the case. False alarms are logged to help us improve the hold-to-confirm mechanism, but there is no penalty for the user.

The 2-second hold-to-confirm design prevents most accidental triggers. The 10-second cancellation window catches the ones that slip through.

Q7How long until someone actually arrives at my door?›

The app shows you a live estimated arrival time based on the assigned team's current GPS position and the route to you. This updates in real time as the team moves — if they encounter a blocked road and reroute, the ETA updates accordingly and you receive a notification explaining the change.

Physical arrival time depends entirely on distance, flood conditions, and available rescue capacity in your area — we do not promise a fixed window because flood geography varies enormously. What we do guarantee: your alert reaches a rescue team within seconds of being sent, and you are never left without a status update for more than a few minutes. If your rescue is delayed, you will know why.

Q8The app says a rescuer is coming but it has been 2 hours and no one has arrived. What do I do?›

First, check the live status screen — it shows the rescuer's current GPS position. If the dot has not moved for more than 15 minutes, the system has already automatically triggered an alert to that rescuer and to the command center. If no response comes within 5 more minutes, the system auto-reassigns your case to the nearest available team and you receive a notification: "A new rescue team has been assigned to you."

If you are in immediate danger and the situation has changed since you sent the original SOS, you can update your case with a new note — tap "Update my situation" from the status screen to add details like rising water level or worsening health condition. This escalates your case priority automatically.

You can also use the in-app messaging channel to send a message directly to the assigned rescuer, or tap the emergency call button to reach the command center operator by phone.

Q9Can my family in another city see that I am safe after the rescue?›

Yes — this is a core feature. Any family member you have designated as an emergency contact in your profile receives an automatic push notification and SMS the moment the rescue team marks your case as completed: "[Your name] has been reached by a rescue team and is confirmed safe." The notification includes the timestamp and the rescuer's name.

Your family can also follow the rescue in real time from their own phone — they see your live location, the rescuer's position moving toward you, and the status updates as they happen. They do not need to call anyone to find out what is going on. Closing the anxiety loop for family members is something we treat as equally important as the rescue itself.

Tap "Accept this case" to claim it. The case is immediately locked to you and removed from the queue — no other rescuer can accept the same victim. Your map updates to show the optimized route to the victim's location, avoiding roads marked as flooded or impassable. You are now the responsible rescuer for this case until you mark it complete or release it.

Q2What information do I see about the victim when I accept a case?›

The case card shows: the victim's full name and profile photo (so you can confirm identity on arrival), their live GPS location on the map with a last-updated timestamp, household details (number of people, whether any are elderly, children under 12, or have medical conditions), and any free-text notes they submitted with their SOS ("We are on the second floor, grandmother cannot walk").

You also see the victim's phone number so you can call them directly if needed, and an in-app messaging channel to send them a message without leaving the app. If the SOS was submitted by a proxy (family member on their behalf), that is clearly labelled so you know the information is secondhand and may be less precise.

Q3I'm on the way to one victim and I can see another victim very close by on the map. Can I pick them up too?›

Yes — and the app is specifically designed to make this easy. While you are en route, the map shows all nearby unassigned SOS pins as orange markers. You can tap any of them to view the case detail and accept it from your current screen without navigating away from your route. If you accept a second case, your route updates to show the most efficient order to reach both victims.

This decision is always yours — the system shows you what is nearby but never forces additional cases on you. You know your boat capacity, the water depth, and the urgency on the ground better than any algorithm does. If you cannot safely take another case, leave it in the queue for another team.

Q4I arrived at the location but the victim is not here — what do I do?›

Tap "Victim not found at this location" from the case screen. This opens a short form asking for the reason (address incorrect, victim already evacuated, GPS was inaccurate) and lets you add a note about what you found. The case is immediately returned to the open queue with a priority escalation flag and a note for the next team: "Previous dispatch unsuccessful — location may be inaccurate."

The system also sends an automatic prompt to the victim's phone asking them to re-confirm or correct their location. If the victim's phone is reachable, they can update their pin in real time so the next rescuer goes to the right place. Your dispatch record is logged accurately — a "not found" outcome never counts against you, and the full context is preserved for the command center.

Q5The road I was going to take is now blocked. How do I report it and get a new route?›

Tap the "Report blockage" button on your active route screen — it appears as a warning icon in the navigation bar. Select the blockage type from the dropdown (bridge down, road flooded, road collapsed, debris blocking) and confirm. Within seconds, three things happen simultaneously: the blockage is posted to the live map for all users in the area, your route is recalculated via Google Maps with the new obstacle excluded, and the command dashboard is notified.

If no alternative route exists to your assigned victim, the system automatically flags the case as "No ground route available" on the command dashboard, triggering the operator to consider alternative dispatch (boat from a different entry point, helicopter, or another team approaching from a different direction).

Q6How do I close a case after completing a rescue?›

Tap one of three outcome buttons on the case screen: "Rescued — victim is safe," "Victim requires additional medical help" (escalates to the command center for medical dispatch), or "Victim not found." No text is required — one tap closes the case. An optional notes field lets you add context for the record ("Four people evacuated, one with ankle injury, taken to Trường Tiểu Học Bình An shelter").

The moment you tap "Rescued," the victim's family receives an automatic notification, the case is closed on the command dashboard, and your status returns to "Available" — you immediately appear as a free resource for the next assignment. The full case record including your GPS trail, timestamps, and outcome is stored permanently for post-event reporting.

Navigation & Field Conditions

Q7Does the navigation know which roads are flooded, or does it just use Google Maps normally?›

Our routing uses the Google Maps API but with a flood exclusion layer on top. Roads that have been marked as flooded or impassable — by government data, IoT sensors, or field reports from other rescuers — are passed to the routing API as exclusions, so they are not included in calculated routes. The result is a path that avoids known flood zones, not just the shortest path.

The map also shows water depth indicators at sensor-equipped locations: color-coded by passability (vehicle passable in blue, boat only in amber, impassable in red). When depth data is not available for a road, it is shown as unknown — never silently assumed to be safe. You always know what you're navigating on.

Q8The app is showing me a route but I can see with my own eyes that the road is passable. Can I override the map?›

Absolutely — you are the ground truth, not the map. You can dismiss the suggested route and navigate manually at any time. If you find that a road the map marked as flooded is actually passable, you can tap "Mark this road as passable" to update the map for other rescuers in real time. Your ground-level observation immediately improves the routing data for every other team in the area.

The map is a decision aid, not an authority. Field judgment always takes precedence — and your field corrections make the map more accurate for the next team, the next event, and the next flood season.

Q9My phone keeps showing me as stationary on the command dashboard even though I'm moving. What's wrong?›

This usually means GPS signal is temporarily lost or degraded — common in areas with heavy cloud cover, dense tree canopy, or near large metal structures like flood barriers. The app will show your last confirmed position until a new GPS fix is acquired. Check that location permissions for the app are set to "Always allow" in your phone settings, and that you are not in low-power mode, which can restrict background GPS.

If the problem persists, open the app, go to your status screen, and tap "Refresh my location" — this forces a new GPS acquisition. If GPS remains unavailable, the app falls back to cell tower triangulation, which is less precise but keeps your dot on the map. The command dashboard will show your position with a lower-confidence indicator so operators know the accuracy is reduced.

Scaling — From MVP to National Deployment

Questions about what happens when the system grows from hundreds of users to tens of thousands, across multiple provinces and disaster types.

Technical Scaling

Q1The MVP handles hundreds of users. What changes architecturally when you need to handle 50,000 concurrent users across three provinces during a major flood?›

The core architecture is already designed for horizontal scale — this was a deliberate early decision. The specific changes required to go from hundreds to 50,000 concurrent users:

1
Infrastructure migration to AWS / GCP — moving from Render to a multi-AZ cloud deployment with auto-scaling groups. Node.js instances scale horizontally behind a load balancer; PostgreSQL moves to a managed RDS with read replicas; Redis moves to ElastiCache cluster mode.Effort: ~2 weeks. No code changes required.
2
Socket.io multi-node with Redis adapter — at scale, multiple Node.js instances cannot share WebSocket connections without a coordination layer. Redis pub/sub acts as the message bus so a client connected to instance A receives events generated on instance B. This is a configuration change in Socket.io, not an architectural change.Redis adapter: ~1 day to implement and test.
3
Geographic sharding — at 3+ provinces, we partition the data by geographic zone. Each province's SOS queue, rescue assignments, and map data are handled by a dedicated database shard. Cross-province queries (national overview for the ministry) run against a read-only aggregation layer, not the operational shards.Sharding implementation: ~3–4 weeks engineering.
4
CDN for map tiles — at scale, serving map tiles from the application server is a bottleneck. We move static tile serving to a CDN (CloudFront or equivalent) so map load is entirely off the application infrastructure.CDN setup: ~1 day.

Total engineering time to go from current MVP to 50,000-user production: approximately 6–8 weeks with a team of 3 engineers. The application code does not change — only infrastructure configuration and the Socket.io adapter.

Q2How does the database handle 10,000 GPS location writes per minute during peak flood?›

Location writes are the highest-volume operation in the system at scale. We handle this with a two-tier write strategy. Tier 1 — Redis (hot data): every incoming GPS update overwrites the current position key for that user in Redis. This is an O(1) operation; Redis handles 100,000+ writes per second without breaking a sweat. The live map is served entirely from Redis — rescuers see the current position, not a database query result.

Tier 2 — PostgreSQL (cold data): location history is written to PostgreSQL asynchronously via a write queue, batched every 10 seconds. Instead of 10,000 individual inserts per minute, the database receives roughly 60 batch writes per minute. This keeps PostgreSQL under comfortable load while retaining the full position history for audit, post-event analysis, and the movement-detection logic.

At 10,000 GPS writes per minute, this architecture keeps PostgreSQL CPU below 30% on a mid-range RDS instance (db.t3.medium). We have headroom to 3–4x that load before needing to scale the database tier.

Q3What happens to system performance when 500 rescuers and 2,000 victims are all on the map simultaneously in the same province?›

This is the scenario we designed Socket.io rooms for. Instead of broadcasting every location update to every connected client — which at 2,500 users would mean millions of messages per minute — we use geographic zone rooms. A rescuer in Quảng Nam province only receives updates for users within their operational zone, not Hà Tĩnh or An Giang.

The command dashboard for a provincial operator receives aggregated updates for their province only. The national ministry dashboard receives province-level summaries (total SOS count, total rescues, active teams) rather than individual GPS streams — this is a separate low-frequency data feed, not a firehose of 2,500 individual positions.

In practice, a province-level event with 500 rescuers and 2,000 victims in a single zone is the worst-case scenario our current architecture is designed for. Beyond that — a truly national event — we would activate geographic sharding and additional Socket.io nodes per province. This is a scale-out operation, not a redesign.

Q4How do you prevent the system from becoming a single point of failure for rescue coordination across an entire province?›

Three principles. First, device-side resilience: every rescuer's active assignment and every victim's queued SOS is cached on the device. If the server goes down entirely, rescuers can continue navigating to their assigned victim using the last known position and cached map tiles. They are not immediately helpless.

Second, no exclusive dependency: the system is built as an enhancement to, not a replacement for, existing civil defense radio and phone coordination. Government rescue teams are trained and equipped to operate without our platform. Our outage means degraded coordination, not zero coordination.

Third, fast recovery: on AWS with auto-scaling and health checks, a failed instance is replaced automatically within 60–90 seconds. Our disaster recovery target (RTO) is under 5 minutes for a single-instance failure, under 15 minutes for a full availability zone failure. We maintain a hot standby in a separate AZ that receives synchronous writes — failover is automatic with no data loss.

Operational & Geographic Scaling

Q5If you expand to 10 provinces, how do you manage 10 different government agencies all with different admin setups?›

We use a federated multi-tenant architecture. Each province operates as an isolated tenant: their own admin accounts, their own verified rescuer roster, their own SOS queue, and their own data — completely separated at the database level from every other province. A Quảng Ngãi admin cannot see An Giang data, and vice versa.

The national Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Development (if they adopt the system) gets a read-only aggregation dashboard showing national-level statistics — total active events, total people rescued, province-by-province response metrics — without accessing any individual province's operational data. This respects both data sovereignty and the administrative hierarchy of Vietnam's disaster response structure.

Onboarding a new province is a configuration operation: create the tenant, set up their admin accounts, configure their geographic zone boundaries, and they are live. We target under 1 business day to onboard a new provincial deployment once the government agreement is signed.

Q7How do you scale the human side — who trains and supports 10,000 rescuers across 10 provinces?›

We use a train-the-trainer model, not direct training at scale. For each province, we train 3–5 "super users" — typically the provincial civil defense IT officer and senior team leaders — who then train their own teams. Our training package includes: a 2-hour facilitator guide, a simulated flood exercise that runs on a test environment with seeded victim data, and a quick-reference card for rescuers that fits on a laminated A5 sheet.

Ongoing support is tiered: in-app help documentation handles common questions, a provincial super user handles team-level issues, and our support team handles platform-level incidents. We target a ratio of 1 support engineer per 5 active provincial deployments — scalable because most issues are user error caught at the super user level, not platform bugs.

Q8What does the system look like in 3 years at full scale — and what are the biggest risks to getting there?›

The 3-year vision: deployed across Vietnam's 10 most flood-vulnerable provinces (Miền Trung coastline + Mekong Delta), integrated with VNMHA's official flood warning system, with a predictive risk layer trained on 3 flood seasons of historical data, and the drone relay system productized and deployed in partnership with civil defense aviation units.

The three biggest risks to getting there:

1. Government procurement speed. Vietnam's public sector procurement cycle is 12–24 months from first conversation to signed contract. We are building relationships now, but the timeline is not in our control. Mitigation: pursue NGO and international humanitarian organization pilots in parallel, which move faster and build the evidence base that government procurement requires.

2. Connectivity infrastructure in remote areas. Our system depends on mobile network coverage that simply does not exist in parts of the Central Highlands and remote Mekong Delta communities. The drone relay addresses this partially, but terrain and population density make some areas genuinely hard to serve digitally. Mitigation: honest scoping of which areas we can serve well, and active partnership with telecoms on emergency network expansion plans.

3. Sustained funding before revenue. The gap between MVP and first government contract is 12–18 months of operating costs with no paying customers. We are addressing this through seed investment and international grant funding (UNDP, ADB disaster resilience programs). The risk is real — we manage it through lean team structure and shared infrastructure costs with partner NGOs.
